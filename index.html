01 — Chemotherapy Scheduling

The patient journey, timed twice

Phase I (132 patients) measured the chemotherapy day end-to-end and surfaced where time was being lost. Phase II (332 patients) re-measured the same six steps after operational changes went in. Below is each step, desired vs. actual, both rounds.

Total turnaround time fell, even with the heavier caseload

Cash-paying patients saw the biggest single gain — discharge duration dropped from 3.0h to 1.48h, more than halved. That alone pulled overall TAT down even though arrival-to-admission wait actually lengthened as volume tripled.

Cash TAT 8.1h → 7.16h −11.6%
Credit TAT 8.37h → 7.12h −14.9%

⛔Where the time was going (Phase I diagnosis)

  • Initial drug-chart preparation delayed the start of almost every patient's wait clock.
  • Bed crunch: on an average morning, only 34 of 39 beds were actually workable.
  • Discharge slowed by delays in PAT and in preparing/receiving the final discharge summary & bill.
  • Insurance discharges requested after 9 PM routinely became next-day stay-backs — billing staff thinned out by then.

✅What was recommended

  • Add transit beds and night-shift manpower specifically for evening/overnight discharges.
  • Get MS Office support looped in to nudge doctor teams and shrink arrival-to-admission time.
  • Fast-track the discharge-summary workflow so billing isn't the last domino.
  • Clinical care flagged an equipment gap: infusion pumps & cardiac monitors needed urgently.
02 — Patient Survey, n=150

Who actually walks through the door

Demographics and referral patterns from the experience survey, run independently of the scheduling study.

Age skew

Senior patients (60+) are the single largest age band, at 42.2% of all cases.

Where patients travel from

Hyderabad alone supplies 40% of footfall; 14.7% of patients travelled internationally.

Referral concentration

One doctor — Dr. Vijay Anand Reddy — accounts for 18% of all patients on his own; 15 doctors were captured in total.

Acquisition

Walk-ins are the dominant channel — up to 63% of footfall — and >90% of walk-ins arrive already carrying a specific doctor's name. Digital channels get used during research, but stay a secondary touchpoint.

PSU mix

Among the small PSU patient segment (n=14), NMDC leads admissions with 6 patients — 42.9% of that group.

03 — Patient Survey, n=150

Why patients choose Apollo — and when they look elsewhere

The cost perception gap

65.1% of patients flag cost as a pre-visit concern, but only 7.2% actually consider switching hospitals because of price. The anxiety is mostly about clarity, not affordability.

Why Apollo, specifically

Patients consistently cite a combination of three things — none stands alone:

When patients do look elsewhere, why?

60.8% of those who considered another hospital did so for a second opinion; 32% cited location feasibility — mostly international and out-of-Hyderabad patients.

04 — Patient Survey, n=150

Experience & satisfaction, in patients' own words

Is care well-coordinated across departments?

The 9% (14 patients) who said no pointed mainly to billing handoffs and unclear communication.

New vs. returning patients

Both groups rate waiting time low — it's a shared pain point. But first-time visitors specifically rate billing clarity lower than returning patients, who've already learned how the process works.

What patients ask for, unprompted

47% of open-ended suggestions are about faster processes and shorter waits. The remainder clusters around two themes: clearer communication and cost transparency.

05 — Patient Survey, n=150

Loyalty & advocacy: the NPS picture

49.3 Overall NPS

The global healthcare benchmark for NPS sits between 30 and 50; anything above 50 is considered excellent. Apollo Oncology lands at 49.3 — solidly inside the strong-benchmark band, just shy of "excellent."

Where detractors cluster

Almost entirely based in Hyderabad & Telangana, and predominantly 45+. Out-of-town and international patients are disproportionately satisfied.

What detractors say

They rate doctor communication highly — but score every other service dimension lower than promoters do.

06 — Insights & Action

What drives recommendation — and what to do about it

A regression model on survey responses ranked five factors by how strongly each predicts a patient recommending Apollo to someone else.

Six insights worth sitting with

Recommended next moves

Three action points from the survey team

01

Make "Apollo Second Opinion" a flagship service

Stop treating it as a fallback option. Build a brand narrative around clinical validation and complex-case resolution, promote a digital second-opinion path, and follow up with every patient who seeks one.

02

Re-sequence the brand message

Lead with clinical excellence, doctor credibility, and patient testimonials. Let technology support that claim rather than open with it. And shift cost conversations from price to value — the anxiety is about clarity, not affordability.

03

Proactively enrol patients 45+ into Apollo Assist

This is the group most likely to become a detractor. Reach them by WhatsApp or SMS before their visit, and have an Apollo Assist SPOC meet them at the entrance.
